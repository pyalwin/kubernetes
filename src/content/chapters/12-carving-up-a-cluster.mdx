Everything in Part II has assumed one app's point of view. Real clusters host
*many* apps from *many* teams, and that raises a question we've dodged until now:
how do you share one cluster without the tenants stepping on each other?

The naive answer — a separate cluster per team — is simple but wasteful. Each
cluster needs its own control plane, its own spare capacity, its own upgrades to
babysit. Ten teams becomes ten of everything. Most organizations would rather run
*one* big cluster and slice it up.

But a single flat cluster has its own problems. Two teams both want a Deployment
called `web` — now their names collide. One team's experiment spawns a thousand
Pods and starves everyone else. A junior engineer's `kubectl delete` hits the
wrong objects because everything lives in one undivided space. Sharing without
boundaries is chaos.

So Kubernetes gives you a way to carve one physical cluster into logical
partitions, each with its own scope for names and its own boundary for policy.
That partition is the **namespace**, and paired with the resource controls in the
back half of this chapter, it's what makes multi-tenancy safe. We'll take the two
in turn: first the *spatial* boundary (namespaces), then the *compute* boundary
(requests, limits, and quotas).

</Section>

A **namespace** is a virtual cluster inside your cluster. Most objects you've met
— Pods, Deployments, Services, ConfigMaps, Secrets, PVCs — live *inside* a
namespace, and a name only has to be unique *within* its namespace. So `web` in
`team-a` and `web` in `team-b` are two entirely separate Services that never
collide. The DNS scheme from Chapter 9 reflects this directly:
`web.team-a.svc.cluster.local` versus `web.team-b.svc.cluster.local`.

Beyond names, a namespace is the natural handle to attach **policy** to. **RBAC**
rules grant a team access to *their* namespace and nothing else, so team-a's
engineers simply can't touch team-b's objects. Resource quotas (next section)
attach per namespace. It's the unit of isolation, organization, and blast-radius
control all at once.

A few objects are deliberately *not* namespaced because they're cluster-wide by
nature: **Nodes**, **PersistentVolumes**, and namespaces themselves. That makes
sense — a node isn't owned by one team, and a PV is a cluster-level resource that
gets *claimed* into a namespace by a PVC.

Out of the box you get a few namespaces: `default` (where objects land if you
don't specify one), `kube-system` (the control-plane components themselves —
CoreDNS, kube-proxy configs, and friends), and `kube-public`. In practice you
create a namespace per team or per environment and wire RBAC and quotas to each.

<Section

  title="Requests and limits: claiming a slice of compute"
  brief={{

    options: [
      { text: "The request is the maximum it may use; the limit is the minimum it's promised." },
      { text: "The request is what the scheduler reserves for placement; the limit is the hard runtime ceiling enforced via cgroups.", correct: true },
      { text: "They're two words for the same value." },

>

Namespaces carve up *names*, but they don't carve up *compute*. Every Pod still
lands on some shared node, and by default a container can grab as much CPU and
memory as the machine has — so one runaway process can starve every other Pod on
the node. The fix is to make each container declare its appetite with two numbers.

A **request** is what the container is *guaranteed*. It's the amount of CPU and
memory the **scheduler** sets aside when deciding where the Pod goes. Recall the
scheduler from Chapter 6: it only places a Pod on a node where the sum of all
requests still fits. So requests are fundamentally about **placement** — they're
the reservation that keeps a node from being oversubscribed. A Pod requesting
`500m` CPU won't be put on a node with only `200m` left.

A **limit** is the *hard ceiling* the container may never exceed at runtime, and
it's enforced by the Linux **cgroups** from way back in our container chapters.
Here a crucial asymmetry shows up:

- Exceed a **memory** limit and the container is **OOM-killed** — terminated and
  restarted. Memory can't be throttled, so the kernel's only option is to kill.
- Exceed a **CPU** limit and the container is **throttled** — slowed down, made to
  wait for cycles — but *not* killed. CPU is compressible; you can always give a
  process less of it.

<Callout type="note">
The two numbers map cleanly onto the two components: **requests → the scheduler**
(where does this Pod fit?), **limits → cgroups on the node** (how much may it
actually consume?). Set requests too low and nodes get oversubscribed and
thrash; set them too high and you waste capacity. Tuning them well is most of the
day-to-day work of running an efficient cluster.
</Callout>

</Section>

    options: [
      { text: "A LimitRange on each container." },
      { text: "A ResourceQuota on the team-a namespace, capping its aggregate requests and limits.", correct: true },
      { text: "A namespace alone enforces it automatically." },
    ],

>

Per-container requests and limits are powerful but *voluntary* — a developer can
set them low, high, or forget them entirely. To actually govern a shared cluster,
a platform team needs to impose rules at the namespace level. Two objects do that,
and they operate at different scopes.

A **ResourceQuota** caps the **whole namespace's** budget. It sets hard ceilings
on the *aggregate* across every Pod in the namespace: total `requests.cpu`, total
`requests.memory`, total limits, and even object counts like "no more than 20
Pods" or "no more than 5 Services." Once team-a's namespace hits its quota, new
Pods that would exceed it are simply rejected at creation. This is how you promise
that team-a can never consume more than its allotted share, no matter how many
Pods it launches. Look back at the quota in the chapter's terminal: `requests.cpu`
used `1200m` of a hard `4`.

A **LimitRange** operates one level down, on **individual containers** within a
namespace. It can set **default** requests and limits (so a Pod created *without*
them still gets sensible values instead of unbounded access) and enforce **min and
max** bounds per container (so nobody requests a ridiculous `64Gi` or omits limits
to dodge throttling). Where a ResourceQuota governs the total, a LimitRange
governs each piece.

Used together, they let a platform team hand a namespace to a product team with
guardrails already installed: "here's your slice — a quota you can't exceed and
sane container defaults you can't accidentally skip." Combined with namespaces for
naming and RBAC for access, that's the full multi-tenancy story: many teams, one
cluster, no collisions and no starvation.

And that closes Part II. You started with a single container and a manual restart
loop; you now have the whole core of Kubernetes — the control plane, Pods,
controllers that keep them alive and update them safely, stable networking,
externalized config, durable storage, and a cluster carved up so many teams can
share it. Every piece turned out to be the same idea wearing a different hat: a
control loop closing the gap between what you declared and what is.

Namespaces and resource governance are where running Kubernetes shifts from
deploying one app to operating a shared platform.

- **Kubernetes Documentation — *Namespaces*, *Resource Quotas*, and *Limit
  Ranges*.** The reference for partitioning a cluster and bounding its compute.
  `kubernetes.io/docs/concepts/policy/`
- **Kubernetes Documentation — *Managing Resources for Containers*.** How requests
  and limits map to scheduling decisions and cgroup enforcement, including the
  OOM-kill vs. CPU-throttle behavior.
- **Kubernetes Documentation — *Quality of Service for Pods*.** How the
  relationship between a Pod's requests and limits sorts it into Guaranteed,
  Burstable, or BestEffort under node pressure.

This is the end of Part II. Part III leaves the core behind and turns to operating
real workloads — scheduling controls, health checks and probes, autoscaling, and
the security model that decides who and what may do what in your cluster.
