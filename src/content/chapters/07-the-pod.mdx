In Chapter 6 we left a Pod at the kubelet's doorstep without saying what one is.
Here's the surprise: the smallest thing you can deploy in Kubernetes is *not* a
container. It's a **Pod** — a wrapper around one or more containers that are
always scheduled together on the same node and share parts of their environment.

That sounds like needless indirection until you hit the problem it solves.
Imagine your web app writes logs to a file, and you want a second program — a log
shipper — to read that file and forward it elsewhere. Those two programs must run
on the *same machine* (they share a file) and must come and go *together* (a log
shipper with no app is pointless). If Kubernetes only knew about containers, you'd
have no way to say "these two are a unit; never split them." You'd cross your
fingers and hope the scheduler put them together.

The Pod is the word for "these belong together." Everything in a Pod is placed on
one node, started and stopped as a group, and given a shared context to
collaborate through. Most Pods hold exactly one container — the wrapper costs you
nothing in that common case — but when you genuinely need two processes glued
into one deployable unit, the Pod is the only thing that expresses it.

<Callout type="tip">
A useful instinct: one container per Pod is the default, and you should reach for
a second container only when it exists *purely to support the first* on the same
machine. "Two unrelated services" is two Pods, not one.
</Callout>

</Section>

  title="What a Pod's containers share"

  quiz={{

    options: [
      { text: "Through the Pod's Service DNS name." },
      { text: "Over localhost — they share one network namespace and one IP, so they coordinate ports like processes on a single host.", correct: true },
      { text: "They can't; containers in a Pod are isolated from each other." },
    ],
  }}
>

Calling two containers "a unit" only means something if they can actually
cooperate. A Pod gives them two concrete things to share.

**One network namespace.** The whole Pod gets a single IP address. Every
container inside sees the same network interface and reaches the others over
`localhost`. So the log shipper can scrape `localhost:8080` for metrics, and the
app and shipper talk with zero network hops. The flip side: they share a port
space. Two containers in one Pod cannot both listen on port 80, exactly as two
processes on one host can't. From *outside*, the world addresses the Pod's IP, not
the individual containers.

**Shared volumes.** A volume declared on the Pod can be mounted into several of
its containers. The app writes logs to `/var/log/app` on a shared volume; the
shipper mounts the same volume and reads them. This is how containers that share
nothing else exchange data — through a patch of filesystem they both see.

What they *don't* share is just as important: each container still has its own
filesystem root (its own image), its own process space by default, and its own
resource limits. The Pod shares the *connective tissue* — address and storage —
while keeping each container's code and dependencies separate. That's the precise
amount of sharing the log-shipper problem needed, and no more.

</Section>

<Section

    takeaway: "The pause container is the anchor that makes a Pod's shared identity outlive any one container restart.",
  }}
  quiz={{
    question: "What is the job of the 'pause' container that Kubernetes silently runs in every Pod?",
    options: [
      { text: "It runs your application's main process." },
      { text: "It holds the Pod's network namespace open so the shared IP survives even when app containers restart.", correct: true },
      { text: "It pauses the Pod during a rolling update." },
    ],
  }}
>

There's a quiet mechanism that makes the shared network actually robust. If the
Pod's IP lived inside your app container, then every time that container crashed
and restarted, the Pod would get a new address — and the "stable shared network"
promise would be a lie.

So Kubernetes runs a nearly invisible helper in every Pod: the **pause
container**. It does almost nothing — it starts, holds open the network namespace
(and a few others), and sleeps. Your real containers *join* its namespace rather
than owning one. Now your app can crash and restart all day; the pause container
keeps holding the namespace, so the Pod's IP never moves. You rarely see it, but
it's why a Pod's identity outlives the churn of its containers.

The pattern of putting a helper container next to your main one is called a
**sidecar**. The log shipper from earlier is the classic example. Others you'll
meet constantly:

- A **proxy** sidecar that handles TLS or routing for the app (the heart of a
  service mesh).
- A **config-sync** sidecar that pulls updated configuration into a shared volume.
- An **adapter** sidecar that translates the app's metrics into a format your
  monitoring expects.

Each works only because of the two kinds of sharing from the last section: the
sidecar reaches the app over `localhost` and exchanges files through a shared
volume. The Pod isn't an arbitrary grouping — it's exactly the unit the sidecar
pattern requires.

</Section>

<Section
  id="lifecycle"
  title="A Pod's lifecycle"
  brief={{
    problem: "A Pod isn't simply 'on' or 'off' — it's pulling images, starting, maybe finishing, maybe failing. You need to read its state to operate it.",
    mechanism: "A Pod moves through phases — Pending, Running, Succeeded, Failed — and a restartPolicy governs what happens when a container inside exits.",
    takeaway: "The phase tells you where a Pod is in its life; the restartPolicy tells you what happens next when something exits.",
  }}
  quiz={{
    question: "A Pod shows STATUS Pending for a while. What does that most likely mean?",
    options: [
      { text: "The Pod's containers have all exited successfully." },
      { text: "The Pod is accepted but not yet running — still waiting to be scheduled or pulling its images.", correct: true },
      { text: "The Pod has permanently failed." },
    ],
  }}
>

Operating Pods means reading their state, so learn the vocabulary. A Pod reports
a high-level **phase**:

- **Pending** — the Pod is accepted by the cluster but not yet running. It might
  be waiting for the scheduler to place it, or the kubelet might be pulling its
  images. A Pod stuck Pending is one of the most common things you'll debug.
- **Running** — the Pod is bound to a node and at least one container has started.
- **Succeeded** — all containers exited cleanly (`0`) and won't restart. Typical
  for one-shot Jobs, not long-running servers.
- **Failed** — all containers terminated and at least one exited non-zero.

Alongside the phase, each Pod carries a **restartPolicy** — `Always` (the default,
for servers you want kept alive), `OnFailure`, or `Never` — that tells the kubelet
what to do when a container *inside* the Pod exits. Note the asymmetry: the
restartPolicy restarts containers within an existing Pod, but it never moves the
Pod to another node. Try inspecting a Pod's phase, containers, and logs:

<Section
  id="never-bare"
  title="Why you rarely create a bare Pod"
  brief={{
    problem: "A Pod you create by hand has nobody watching it. If its node dies, the Pod dies with it — and nothing brings it back.",
    mechanism: "The restartPolicy restarts containers, but a Pod is never rescheduled by itself. A controller is what replaces a lost Pod.",
    takeaway: "Bare Pods are mortal and final; you almost always want a controller wrapping them so they self-heal.",
  }}
  quiz={{
    question: "You create a bare Pod (no controller) and the node it's on dies. What happens to the Pod?",
    options: [
      { text: "It is automatically recreated on a healthy node." },
      { text: "It's gone — nothing reschedules a bare Pod, because no controller is watching it.", correct: true },
      { text: "Its restartPolicy moves it to another node." },
    ],
  }}
>

Here's the catch that sends you to the next chapter. A Pod you create directly —
a "bare" Pod — has *nobody watching it*. The restartPolicy can restart a crashed
container within the Pod, but if the whole Pod is lost — its node reboots, fails,
or is drained — there is no loop anywhere whose job is to bring it back. It simply
vanishes. The restartPolicy restarts containers; it does not resurrect Pods.

That fragility is by design. Remember the control loop from Chapter 1:
self-healing comes from a *controller* watching desired state and acting on gaps.
A bare Pod has no controller, so it has no desired-state guardian, so it doesn't
self-heal. It's a one-off, and one-offs die for good.

So in practice you almost never write a Pod manifest by hand. Instead you create
a **controller** — a Deployment, StatefulSet, Job, or DaemonSet — and *it* creates
and watches the Pods for you. Treating Pods as disposable is the whole point:
when one dies, the controller notices the gap between "should be 3" and "are 2"
and makes a fresh Pod, possibly on a different node. You stopped caring about
individual Pods and started declaring how many you want.

<Callout type="note">
This is the cattle-not-pets idea made concrete. You don't name, nurse, or mourn
individual Pods. You declare a count and a template; the controller keeps that
true. Which raises the obvious question — *how* does a controller keep N copies
alive? That's Chapter 8.
</Callout>

<Section
  id="sources"
  title="Sources & further reading"
>

The Pod is the most-explained object in Kubernetes for good reason — it's the
atom everything else is built from.

- **Kubernetes Documentation — *Pods* and *Pod Lifecycle*.** The authoritative
  description of shared namespaces, phases, and restart policies.
  `kubernetes.io/docs/concepts/workloads/pods/`
- **Kubernetes Documentation — *Sidecar Containers*.** The pattern and its
  first-class support, including native sidecar startup ordering.
- **Burns, Oppenheimer, *Design Patterns for Container-Based Distributed Systems*
  (HotCloud, 2016).** The original taxonomy of sidecar, ambassador, and adapter
  patterns that the Pod was built to enable.

Next chapter: we wrap Pods in the controller that keeps a fixed number of them
alive — and then in the controller that lets you swap them out, a few at a time,
without downtime.
