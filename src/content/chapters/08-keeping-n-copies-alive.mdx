We ended the last chapter with a problem: bare Pods don't self-heal. Lose the
node and the Pod is gone for good. What you actually want is to say "three copies
of `web`, always" and never think about it again — through crashes, out-of-memory
kills, and machines that catch fire at 3am.

That is a control loop, and you already know its shape from Chapter 1: observe
actual, compare to desired, act to close the gap, repeat. The thing that runs
this loop for replica counts is the **ReplicaSet**. Its whole job — the entire
job, nothing more — is to make a number true. You tell it `replicas: 3`, hand it
a Pod template, and it spends the rest of its life asking one question: *are
there three healthy Pods that match?* Two? It creates one. Four? It deletes one.
Three? It does nothing and asks again.

This is why "self-healing" isn't a feature someone bolted on. Kill a Pod by hand
and a new one appears in seconds — not because Kubernetes is clever, but because
a loop noticed `2 ≠ 3` and acted. The healing *is* the loop. Once you see the
ReplicaSet as nothing but the replica-count instance of the universal control
loop, it stops being a new concept and becomes the same old one wearing a job
title.

</Section>

>

There's a hole to fill before the count makes sense. The cluster is full of Pods
from many apps. When a ReplicaSet asks "are there three of mine?", how does it
know which Pods are *its*? It can't keep a list of names — the names change every
time a Pod is replaced, which is constantly.

Kubernetes solves this the same way it solves almost every "which objects relate
to which" question: **labels and selectors**. A label is just a key-value tag you
stick on an object — `app: web`, `tier: frontend`, `env: prod`. A selector is a
query over labels. The ReplicaSet carries a selector like `app: web`, and *any*
Pod whose labels match is considered one of its replicas. Ownership is by
membership in a set, not by a pointer to a name.

This loose coupling is quietly powerful. The ReplicaSet doesn't track individual
Pods; it tracks a *population* defined by a query. Replace a Pod and the new one,
stamped with the same `app: web` label from the template, is automatically
counted. It's the difference between "I'm responsible for Pods #7, #8, #9" — which
breaks the moment #7 dies — and "I'm responsible for the set of Pods labeled
`app: web`," which survives any amount of churn.

<Callout type="warning">
Because ownership is by label match, overlapping selectors are a real footgun.
If two controllers both select `app: web`, they'll fight over the same Pods —
each seeing the other's Pods as surplus and deleting them. Keep selectors
distinct, and let controllers manage the labels for you rather than hand-editing
Pod labels in a running set.
</Callout>

</Section>

>

The ReplicaSet is great at one thing and blind to another. It holds *one* Pod
template steady. But that template pins a specific image — `web:v1`. What happens
when you want `web:v2`?

Your bad options, if a ReplicaSet were all you had:

- **Edit the template in place and delete all the Pods.** The ReplicaSet recreates
  them with v2 — but for a few seconds there are *zero* Pods serving traffic. An
  outage on every deploy.
- **Edit the template and wait.** A ReplicaSet doesn't actually replace existing
  Pods when you change its template; it only applies the new template to Pods it
  creates *next*. So nothing happens until Pods happen to die. The rollout is
  nondeterministic and might never finish.
- **And if v2 is broken?** You've destroyed the v1 Pods. There's no saved copy of
  the old working state to fall back to. Rollback means redeploying v1 by hand and
  hoping.

The missing capability is *managing change over time*: bring up v2 gradually,
take down v1 in step, keep the service at full strength throughout, and keep the
old version around in case v2 misbehaves. That's not a tweak to the ReplicaSet's
job — it's a whole separate job that *uses* ReplicaSets as its building blocks.
Enter the Deployment.

</Section>

      { text: "It deletes the old ReplicaSet, then creates the new one." },
    ],

>

A **Deployment** sits one level above ReplicaSets and choreographs them. You
don't usually create ReplicaSets yourself; you create a Deployment, and it
creates and manages ReplicaSets for you. The reason for the extra layer is
entirely about handling *change* safely.

When you update a Deployment — say, change the image to `web:v2` — it does not
touch the existing Pods. Instead it creates a **new ReplicaSet** for v2 and
performs a **rolling update**: it scales the new ReplicaSet up a little and the
old one down a little, over and over, until the new one holds all the replicas
and the old one holds zero. Two parameters tune the dance: `maxSurge` (how many
extra Pods it may create above the desired count) and `maxUnavailable` (how many
it may take down at once). With sensible values, your service never drops below
full strength — old Pods keep serving until new ones are `Ready`.

The old ReplicaSet isn't deleted; it's kept around scaled to zero. That detail is
what makes **rollback** trivial: the previous working version is sitting right
there, one command from being scaled back up.

So the stack is three controllers deep, each reconciling toward the layer above:
the **Deployment** says *which version*, a **ReplicaSet** says *how many of one
version*, and **Pods** are the running copies. Change the top, and the change
ripples down safely.

</Section>

    options: [
      { text: "Kubernetes rebuilds v1 from source." },
      { text: "The Deployment scales the retained v1 ReplicaSet back up and the v2 one down — reversing the rolling update.", correct: true },
      { text: "It deletes the whole Deployment and asks you to recreate it." },
    ],

>

A safe rollout mechanism is only useful if you can watch it and reverse it. Both
are first-class.

`kubectl rollout status deployment/web` follows the transition live, printing
progress as old Pods are retired and new ones become ready, and exiting when the
Deployment has fully converged. It's the command you'd put in a deploy script's
"wait until healthy" step, because it blocks until the new version is genuinely
serving.

When v2 turns out to be broken — and it will, eventually — `kubectl rollout undo
deployment/web` reverses the move. Because the v1 ReplicaSet is still sitting at
zero replicas, "undo" simply scales it back up and the v2 one back down, the same
rolling update run in reverse. No rebuild, no scramble; you're back to the last
known-good version in seconds. `kubectl rollout history` shows you the revisions
you can return to.

Step back and notice what you've gained since the bare Pod of Chapter 7. You no
longer name Pods, count them by hand, restart them, or sweat a deploy. You declare
a version and a replica count; a tower of control loops keeps that true and lets
you change your mind safely. This is the Kubernetes promise delivered in full for
*one* app on *one* set of Pods. The remaining problem is that those Pods keep
changing their IP addresses — so how does anything reliably *talk* to them? That's
the next chapter.
