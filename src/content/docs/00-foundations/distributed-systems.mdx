Kubernetes is a **distributed system**: many machines cooperating to behave like
one. That setting forces a particular design, and once you see the pattern you
will recognize it everywhere in the system.

## Desired state vs. actual state

You tell Kubernetes the **desired state** ("5 replicas of this app exist"). The
**actual state** is whatever is true right now ("4 are running, 1 just
crashed"). These drift apart constantly — nodes fail, processes die, traffic
shifts. The job of the system is to keep closing the gap.

## The control loop

The mechanism is the **control loop**, and it never stops:

1. **Observe** the actual state.
2. **Compare** it to the desired state.
3. **Act** to reduce the difference.
4. Repeat, forever.

Almost every component in Kubernetes — the thing that manages replicas, the
thing that manages nodes, the thing that manages endpoints — is one of these
loops, called a **controller**. This is why the system *self-heals*: kill a Pod
and a loop simply notices the gap and recreates it. You did not write recovery
logic; you declared an outcome and the loop maintains it.

## Why declarative beats imperative here

In a system where anything can fail at any moment, *step-by-step* instructions
("create this, then that") are fragile — if a step fails halfway, what now?
*Declarative* desired state is robust: there is no "halfway." The loop just
keeps comparing and acting until reality matches, no matter how it got knocked
off course. This is the philosophical core of everything that follows.

## Fault tolerance

Because state lives in **etcd** (a replicated, consistent store) and work is
driven by always-running loops, the cluster tolerates the failure of individual
nodes and components without losing track of what *should* be true. Reconcile,
recover, repeat.
