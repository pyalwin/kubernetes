One container on one laptop is easy. The trouble starts when you run *many*
containers across *many* machines and need them to stay healthy without you
watching. Who restarts a crashed process at 3am? Who moves work off a dead
node? Who adds copies when traffic spikes and removes them when it fades? Who
wires up networking so a hundred short-lived containers can still find each
other?

Doing all of that by hand does not scale. An **orchestrator** lets you instead
*declare the outcome you want* — "run 5 copies of this app, reachable at this
address" — and then continuously works to make reality match. This idea, called
**desired-state reconciliation**, is the single most important concept in this
whole book. Kubernetes is, at heart, a large collection of loops that each
watch some piece of the world and nudge it back toward what you declared.

## The control plane at a glance

The brain that does this reconciling is the **control plane**. You will meet
every piece of it in Part 1, but here are the two you should know first — click

The **API server** is the front door: everything — you, the components, the
tools — talks to the cluster through it. **etcd** is the cluster's memory: the
single source of truth for what you declared and what currently exists.

## Where we are headed

Before Kubernetes makes sense, the layer beneath it has to: containers, the
Linux features that make them possible, the networking that connects them, and
the distributed-systems ideas that make reconciliation reliable. That is
Part 0. Then we climb.
