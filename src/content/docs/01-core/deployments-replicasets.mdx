In the previous chapter we saw that bare Pods are fragile. The fix is a
**controller** that watches your Pods and keeps the right number alive. For
stateless web apps, that controller is the **Deployment**, and underneath it
sits the **ReplicaSet**. Together they turn "I want my app running" from a chore
into a single declarative statement.

## ReplicaSet: keep N copies alive

A **ReplicaSet** has one job: ensure that a specified number of identical Pod
replicas are running at all times. You give it a `replicas: 3` count, a Pod
template, and a **label selector**. Its control loop constantly compares the
number of Pods matching that selector against the desired count. Too few? It
creates more from the template. Too many? It deletes the extras. Kill a Pod by
hand and a fresh one appears seconds later — that's the ReplicaSet reconciling.

The selector/label relationship matters: the ReplicaSet "owns" any Pod whose
labels match its selector, for example `app: web`. This is loose coupling by
labels rather than hard references, a recurring Kubernetes theme.

## Deployment: manage ReplicaSets for safe change

You rarely create a ReplicaSet directly. Instead you create a **Deployment**,
which manages ReplicaSets *for* you. Why the extra layer? Because change is the
hard part. When you update your app — say a new image tag — the Deployment
doesn't edit existing Pods. It creates a *new* ReplicaSet for the new version and
performs a **rolling update**: scaling the new ReplicaSet up and the old one down
a few Pods at a time, governed by `maxSurge` and `maxUnavailable`. The result is
zero-downtime deploys, because old Pods keep serving until new ones are ready.

Because the old ReplicaSet is kept around (scaled to zero), **rollbacks** are
trivial. If the new version misbehaves, `kubectl rollout undo deployment/web`
scales the previous ReplicaSet back up. You can watch a rollout with
`kubectl rollout status deployment/web` and inspect history with
`kubectl rollout history`.

## The mental model

Think of it as three layers, each declarative: a **Deployment** records *what
version you want*; a **ReplicaSet** records *how many copies of one version*; and
**Pods** are the running instances. Each layer is a controller reconciling
toward the layer above it. You change the top, and the change ripples down safely.
