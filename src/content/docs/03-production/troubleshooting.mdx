Most production incidents are diagnosed with four commands. The skill is not
memorizing flags тАФ it is reading what the cluster is already telling you, in
order, and letting each Pod *state* point you at the cause.

## The four-command playbook

1. **`kubectl get pods`** тАФ the wide-angle view. The `STATUS` and `RESTARTS`
   columns usually name the problem before you do anything else.
2. **`kubectl describe pod <name>`** тАФ the detail view. Scroll to the bottom:
   the **Events** list is a timeline of what the scheduler, kubelet, and
   controllers tried and why it failed.
3. **`kubectl logs <name>`** (add **`--previous`** for a crashed container) тАФ the
   application's own account of its death.
4. **`kubectl get events --sort-by=.lastTimestamp`** тАФ cluster-level events when
   the problem is bigger than one Pod.

## Reading the Pod states

Each unhealthy state points at a *layer*:

- **`CrashLoopBackOff`** тАФ the container starts then exits repeatedly, so the
  kubelet backs off between restarts. The bug is *inside the app*: a missing env
  var, a failed migration, a panic. Go to `logs --previous`.
- **`ImagePullBackOff`** / `ErrImagePull` тАФ the kubelet can't fetch the image.
  Wrong tag, typo'd registry, or missing pull secret. The app never even ran.
- **`Pending`** (unschedulable) тАФ the scheduler can't place the Pod. `describe`
  will say `insufficient cpu/memory` or an unsatisfiable node selector/affinity.
  This is a *capacity or placement* problem, fixed by smaller requests, more
  nodes, or relaxed constraints.
- **`OOMKilled`** тАФ the container exceeded its memory **limit** and the kernel
  killed it. Raise the limit or fix the leak.
